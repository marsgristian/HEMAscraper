DISCOURSE ON THE REASONS, Of sword, and dagger.

[3] (P72.S2)
HAVING THEREFORE, AS WE BELIEVE, TREATED quite fully of the practice and times of the sword alone, it seems very appropriate to us, in order to provide complete satisfaction to anyone who reads, to place in this place the warnings and rules of the sword and dagger, not with the intention of preferring this method of fighting to that of the sword alone, nor to multiply various precepts, departing from those teachings we have previously imparted, but rather to show the richness and abundance of the use of this art, which, being able to combine multiple weapons together, becomes more admirable and more perfect. And thus, not straying from the subtleties of the times of the sword alone, nor contradicting them in any way, as they are more intricate without comparison, we shall pass over them in silence, having proposed and resolved them in their places. Rather, we shall proceed to declare, as far as necessary, the perfect operation of the sword and dagger, and to teach the true knowledge of the techniques useful for offense and defense, and harmful to the inexperienced in the art.

[4] (P72.S3)
And therefore, he who is well acquainted with the times will easily derive great fruit from it, for these two weapons, being one to assist the other, and when united they greatly strengthen according to the need, not the least, for by seeing themselves they share the operations, one in defending and the other in offending. We hope that those who shall exercise themselves in this, observing the present teachings, will attain the perfection they desire.

[5] (P73.S0)
OF THE POSITIONS OF, Sword and Dagger.

[6] (P73.S1)
THE POSTURES OF THE SWORD AND DAGGER ARE FORMED WITH THE BODY CURVED IN A SMALL STEP, AND WITH THE WEIGHT OF THE BODY ON THAT FOOT WHICH MUST REMAIN FIRM, AND ALSO WITH THE DAGGER ARM EXTENDED TO A HEIGHT EQUAL TO THE TIP OF THE SHOULDER, AND WITH THE TIP OF THE DAGGER HELD UPWARD, LOOKING TOWARD THE ENEMY, AND WITH THE SWORD SO ADVANCED THAT IT IS AT LEAST ONE FIFTH OF ITS LENGTH IN FRONT OF THE DAGGER, SO THAT IT CANNOT BE SO DISTURBED BY THE MOVEMENTS AND FEINTS OF THE ENEMY. A MAN IN SUCH A GUARD WILL BE ABLE TO BETTER UTILIZE THE STRENGTH OF IT IN EVERY EVENT, WHICH HE MUST KEEP JOINED WITH THE SWORD AND THE DAGGER TO CLOSE THE WAY IN THE MIDDLE TO THE ENEMY TO STRIKE; BUT HE WHO WISHES TO USE THE ADVANCED SWORD MUST KEEP THE DAGGER JOINED WITH THE GUARD OF IT, SO THAT IT WILL CLOSE THE PLACE TO STRIKE TO THE SAID ENEMY, AND SIMILARLY HE MUST KEEP THE RIGHT SIDE FORWARD AND THE LEFT BACK, FOR THESE WILL BE MORE EXPOSED AND OF GREATER DANGER.

OF THE COUNTERPOSTURES.

[8] (P74.S1)
IV. IT IS DIFFICULT TO FORM THE COUNTERPOSTURES because one must attend to both one’s own weapons and those of the enemy, as well as their postures. Therefore, one must be cautious not to overextend oneself in seeking some advantage, lest the enemy gain the sword with his dagger and strike in such a way that one must keep the sword far enough from him to ensure it can be saved, and to move it before it is seized by the enemy’s dagger. In the rest, one must observe the same circumstances and reasons shown in describing the counterpostures of the sword alone, while ensuring that the body is free from the point of the enemy without any movement of the body or weapons, and that the said weapons are joined for greater strength and defense.

[ 78 ] ON THE FINDING OF THE SWORD: THE FINDING OF THE SWORD, WHICH IS DONE WITH THE SWORD, and dagger is quite different from that of the sword alone, for now one engages with the dagger, now with the sword, and most often with both the sword and dagger together.

[10] (P75.S1)
When one is engaged with the sword alone, it happens that the enemy keeps it so withdrawn that the dagger cannot reach it, and sometimes the same sword cannot penetrate forward due to the danger of the dagger being drawn. Nevertheless, in this case, it suffices to position it in such a way that it closes off the path where the enemy's point watches, and with the strong in such a manner that said enemy's point cannot approach the body, ensuring that the weak is not found with the strong, which usually hinders and diverts the enemy from coming into range. Moreover, one can also, in advancing, lift the sword from that defense and go to strike with the dagger, where the sword was previously held, which will defend very well, and the enemy will be deceived, who very likely would have believed that one intended to parry with the sword, thus easily ruining any plan he had.

[11] (P75.S2)
This is a good way to use when the enemy is withdrawn, for one should not go so far with the sword that it is lost, nor should one go less with the dagger, for there are some who, seeing the enemy's dagger, strike with their own and go to wound, and thus this advance brings much danger. Moreover, when one lets it slide so far, the dagger is easily deceived by the feints and movements of the enemy, which cause it to become disordered. But if the said enemy is advanced, there will not be so much danger; rather, one can easily approach her with the dagger when one knows how to operate correctly, for one should not carry it so high that upon reaching the enemy it must be lowered, nor so low that it needs to be raised. Neither should one make any motion to reach the enemy's point, for even if it were small, it would be suitable to wound the enemy or at least to disturb them, and it would not be without danger. Rather, one must position the dagger with the point in the same line as the enemy, so that by approaching the point, the dagger is acquired without making any other motion with it. And if the line of the said enemy is a little low, one should also begin with the dagger at this low position, but wishing to be secure, the body must lower proportionally, so that it is known that when the enemy attacks, one can easily parry without raising the arm, for if the enemy were to raise, they could deceive by feigning an attack and then retreat, where one would be wounded without being able to defend oneself. But by keeping the arm of the dagger stable, one could easily defend both sides, so that whoever knows how to maintain the proper rule can approach the enemy wherever she may be, provided that this enemy is so advanced that she is longer than her dagger; for if she were more withdrawn, it would be a mistake, because beyond the reasons shown above, one would be in danger of advancing too closely before finding the point of the said enemy, where one would be wounded. Likewise, if she were too low towards the ground, in which case it would be better to cover her with the sword so that the adversary cannot attack from the side of the dagger, or if wishing to attack, would be forced to do so from the side of the sword to free it. But if one wishes to wound, it would be necessary, in making said cover, to have kept the dagger steady, for thus one would have turned the sword from third to second, and the man would have approached with the hand of it joined to that of the dagger, and would have closed that space as much as possible between the two weapons, so that when the same enemy comes, the sword would always find her with the dagger, which would not have been in any other motion. But if the said enemy were low, and so far towards the dagger, one could not cover, and it would be little judgment to go with the point of the sword for the danger that at that time she might attack above the sword and go to wound between one hand and the other, where the path would be clear. And although one could parry with the dagger, nonetheless, such a large motion would be made that if the enemy feigned to wound in that part, she could go to wound in the other, which is uncovered, and offend, besides being able to attack with the point of the sword and go to wound between one hand and the other, sliding the blade, and thus would wound in the side of the dagger, in such a way that one would remain in the same risk. Therefore, it would be better in such a case to close the enemy from outside with the hand low to the right knee, and turned in fourth so that she would be inside, and keep that part more covered, and similarly with the point angled upwards towards the dagger, which would render all that part of the body defended up to the head. And all the more by keeping the dagger joined to the sword, for in this way not only would the sword be closed, but a good counter-posture would be made, nor would there be left to the enemy any other way to offend, namely above the sword, which, being held so angled, and with the left arm forward, could easily be parried with one of the two weapons, or with both together, and with little motion, for between the two weapons there would be no place to wound. And even if the enemy found herself in second, or in first, and the point were in that part still disengaged from the dagger, which would cause danger from below, and in the best case, it would be much better to employ this form and place the point of the sword at the enemy, which would have the same effect. That point must always be kept in measure and in such a form that it is known to be free to strike in time, nor is it meant to keep it free by distancing it greatly from presence, for in such a way it would be kept closed outside before it could be put back. We do not wish to refrain from saying that with the sole sword one can go to confront the enemy in any place, provided that the dagger is held in such a way that it requires little movement to defend that part where the said enemy might come to strike. This manner will indeed be good, and although others may contradict this, who do not wish for both weapons to be engaged at the same time in order to have at least one free to parry and strike according to the situation, and that one should be held for defense and the other for offense, we assert that when two pieces are to serve for both effects, employing them both in defense results in a single action. We concede this, but then we say that such a united defense is not only stronger but also covers more of the other side, where the enemy might come, which, at times, finds itself less exposed and more vulnerable to being struck, and easier for the other to parry. Furthermore, placing oneself in defense with a single weapon is more dangerous, not only to be disturbed but also to be overcome, as often happens when one has taken a defensive stance with a single weapon, and the adversary has changed their intent to strike, finding oneself so disunited and weak that one is forced to engage with all weapons in servitude, such that what one did not wish to do voluntarily is then done under duress when the said enemy has moved, and thus one remains so disturbed and confused that one cannot strike due to the discomfort in which one finds oneself in defense. But conversely, when one engages with both weapons, and the enemy changes to strike or to do something else, the two weapons that were in defense divide in such a change, one of which will parry, and the other will strike due to the union in which they find themselves. Sometimes one can also go to defend with both from the other side and strike at the same time, as the first defense is made strong in relation to the said union of weapons, which has kept the body more covered and has acted voluntarily, not constrained by necessity, such that it can be well understood that the one who engages both weapons will also dispose of them with more judgment and security, in such a way that it will not hinder the striking in time according to the occasion, while the one who is forced to engage will often be impeded, unable to do anything but the simple defense, which, however good it may be, can easily be deceived.

HOW ONE SHOULD ACT AGAINST THE Enemy Positioned in Guard on the Left Foot.

[15] (P76.S1)
WHEN ONE HAS TO ENGAGE WITH ANOTHER, WHO IS SUPPORTED ON THE LEFT FOOT, it must be considered that the enemy's sword is so withdrawn that it is difficult to find it, and that the same enemy holds the dagger very advanced with the intention of finding his adversary and then striking when the time is right. Moreover, most of the time, knowing his line is short, he cannot reach unless he passes, and he knows very well that he cannot pass unless he first gains the aforementioned adversary. It should also be considered that in passing, he will come with great force, and this is due to the considerable distance from the place where he leaves his foot to the place where he intends to stop, and that his sword is not only carried by the arm and the foot but by the whole body, which also comes with great fury to quickly reach the strike. For this reason, he strikes heavily, especially if he is in time, as the other approaches, who cannot then break measure, and thus the counterattack is much more forceful. Furthermore, he said that since the enemy knows he cannot do anything good while remaining at a distance, he always strives to get closer in order to gain the sword and the measure, so that after all these considerations, he must know how to discern the greater advantage and take advantage of that distance, in which he arms himself more quickly than the one who stands on the left foot, provided he keeps the right foot forward. And while the other wishes to gain his position, that is the time to strike him and break measure to keep himself distant, so that the said enemy cannot pass, or if he cannot strike him, at least to make a feint to disturb him, and then strike him, or hold him, and in that time to distance himself enough to remain in the first measure in order to take the time more comfortably, while he returns to move. Because being on the left foot, the flank under the dagger presents great danger, and if he wishes to cover it, he exposes himself above, not being able to cover both places at once, so that finding the time, he could always strike one of the said two places; and each time the sword provokes the dagger to move, he will surely be struck, either on one side or the other, and all the better if the provocation occurs at the time he approaches, when he cannot break measure, and similarly if he does not carry himself so far forward that he must doubt receiving a response greater than the blow as often happens. But when he sees that the enemy approaches to gain the sword with the dagger, then he should keep the point aimed at the enemy's fist, and if the said dagger were extended with the point forward, which hides the fist, then he should keep the sword directed under the edge, in such a way that it is neither inside nor outside, and therefore it would be necessary to have it in a straight line in the third guard. And as the enemy approaches, he should gather the arm close to the body while always maintaining the straight line, so that the sword does not bend in any part and draw the enemy into the first measure, so that his hand penetrates the point of the sword, and at the moment his foot arrives in measure, strike him from the same straight line under the edge of the said dagger, and the closer to the arm, the better. When that exposed part is not given, he should strike above the said dagger, making a slight turn of the hand towards the second guard, but close to the dagger, striving above all to arrive quickly. When the exposed part is inside, he could turn the hand into the fourth guard, which would strike that part, but always close to the edge of the dagger. And it is certain that these strikes would succeed by taking the time when the adversary approaches, and even when his dagger is in such a position that it seems impossible to strike him, he should make a slight motion with the point either inward or outward to make him waver and then strike him. And to better save oneself from the response or counteraction that the said enemy might make, the dagger should be positioned towards the enemy's point in such a way that when it comes, wherever he wishes, this dagger should make little movement in parrying, and in this form situated, it should be held firm to have more ease in defending oneself, but it should not be too advanced, so that the enemy cannot overwhelm him before he is in measure.

[16] (P76.S2)
And when a man is not positioned with such subtlety, and the enemy's dagger begins to penetrate the point from one side or the other, he should not therefore attempt to free himself with the intention of liberating it; rather, he should gradually move it away from the line, as far as the said dagger would follow him, for if the enemy wished to have it, he would move so far out of line with that dagger that he could be wounded. Or, by making a feint, he would remain so disordered by the great opening that he would certainly be wounded at that moment. But if the man does not wish to maintain this style, it is advisable to keep a distant distance with the free sword, somewhat gathered, so that it cannot easily be found by the enemy, and to provoke him with various timings and calls, always remaining in breaking measure, so that when he resolves to strike, and takes some time to pass decisively, the man can save himself according to the quality of the guard. For whoever goes against him, even if he saves himself from the first effect, the parry would nonetheless be so violent that it would prevent the wounding; moreover, if the enemy changes the effect, he would certainly be wounded. Thus, breaking measure as has been said, the enemy's blow would come with force, and it would be easier to parry, and with such convenience that if the enemy were to change the effect while approaching, he would still be able to parry from the other side before it reached him. Therefore, he who is positioned on the right foot should never go to measure the other, who stands on the left, but should keep himself distant and approach to strike when the adversary comes near, taking advantage of the measure, which reaches from farther away. There is another way as well, when one intends to defend himself and break measure in the same stroke, that is to give the sword to the said enemy's dagger, so that, believing he has it at the moment he moves to come, he frees it by breaking measure and strikes. This method is quite subtle and deceptive, operating with the circumspection that is required, so that in thinking to free it, he does not remain deceived by the enemy and wounded, as one must also be wary that the said enemy does not notice such intention in order to better be able to mock him.

HOW TO ACT WHEN FINDING ONESELF ON THE Left AGAINST ONE SITUATED ON THE RIGHT FOOT.

[18] (P77.S1)
HE WHO WISHES TO ADVANCE WITH THE LEFT FOOT BEFORE ASSAILING THE ENEMY SITUATED ON THE RIGHT, WILL KNOW HIS DISADVANTAGE, which is that his sword, wishing to strike and return to guard, does not reach as far as that of the one situated on the right foot, who strikes and easily returns with little movement of the body. Therefore, he must pass with the right foot, and upon passing, he will not be able to return backward due to the great movements of the foot and body, and because in that posture he goes so far beyond that it is impossible to return and break in measure at one time. Thus, he will be compelled, if he wishes to return after having struck, to charge upon the enemy and keep her always bound toward the ground until he has recovered, and this to prevent that enemy from striking him while he retreats.

[19] (P77.S2)
Nevertheless, if it happens that the person on the right is skilled in knowing how to free the sword, this other one would be in danger of being wounded before he has stopped in relation to the long term, which he must return the foot to where he has moved much longer, than it is to move it. Therefore, when the man has passed with the right, it is better to follow also with the left, and to pass completely, or having passed with the right, and having wounded, to immediately return the arms against the enemy, and to cover all that part, where it is found remaining firm with the feet, and if the enemy retreats to free himself, then he can return to wound him again at the time he moves, because from such a tight measure he cannot exit in time; all these reasons are understood after one has wounded, but when approaching in the distances, in which one must wound, one should position the dagger in such a way, that going beyond with the foot, and holding it firm, it is, in reaching the enemy's point, close to its edge without moving in any part, and it is known that, standing firm, he has defended that straight line, which comes from the point to the body, and this is the true way to acquire the measure, and that the enemy cannot easily wound, which if he changes the sword, it will be a certain change made to save it from danger, and that change will provide convenience to wound that enemy in that motion; or if the man is not so in measure, he should approach, but with little motion of the point of that dagger, and cover from the other side in the same way, and although he finds the time to wound, and that he passes, nevertheless he must slide with the said dagger along the enemy's edge without striking it, understanding when that is on the inside, that being on the outside, it should neither strike nor slide along the edge, but leave that dagger in its own place, or a little forward so that one should never penetrate the fourth part of the enemy's sword, and in wounding one must turn the hand in the fourth guard to join it with the dagger, so that both the upper and lower parts are defended at the same time; it is true that sometimes the enemy has in hand the arms so joined that the dagger cannot hold the said rule, and also by some angle that the enemy forms, and therefore knowing that the man finds himself with the left side in front, and that for his safety there remains no other defense, than that of the dagger which is not sufficient to defend from the knee to the head, because it cannot defend more than one place at the same time, and that in approaching in the distances it can be very troubled, and disordered and so much, that if the enemy observes the time, and the advantage of the motion, he could certainly wound him, he must assist himself with the union of the sword, which defends one part, and that dagger the other, which in this way will do great benefit to the body, because the said dagger will be more secure in defense, and greater advantage will have to wound, given that the point of the sword will always be closer to the enemy, nor will it be less safe from the enemy's arms, than it was before; and now the form will be shown with which it should be positioned.

[20] (P77.S3)
Therefore, the man should find himself in the third guard with the point directed towards the point of his own dagger, with the hand extended enough to clearly know the strong part of it, able to defend the flank under the dagger, and with the arm of that dagger extended outward enough to also ensure that nothing is exposed above that arm. He must be accompanied by the sword in such a way that the lower part is defended while standing in guard, and that the enemy cannot strike anywhere else but above the sword outside; he should also stand with the points so closely together that the sword can never be found disengaged from the dagger due to the danger of being wounded regarding that weak point. In summary, the point of the sword should be fortified by the dagger, and the enemy should not be able to make it yield or come to strike. This is the form of guard with which one should proceed, measuring the distances to be able to strike when the opportunity arises, and while advancing, saluting that which is exposed above the sword. The feet should always be outside of the enemy's, keeping the body in line with the left flank forward, the knee bent together with the head above the hilt of the sword, so that if the enemy were to come, one could more easily defend that exposed area above the sword, as mentioned above. For if one bends the body at the hips as one naturally does and holds the sword to defend the left flank, the head would be so far out of line that it would be wounded before one could defend. But by bending it in such a way that it is above the hilt and over the left knee with the right shoulder in, one would make a body escape in such a manner that nothing would protrude beyond the sword. Therefore, if the enemy wished to strike, it would necessarily have to be done at the closest edge of the said sword, thus the defense would be easy. With such a form, one could go against all high or low right foot guards, and with the sword straight or angled, there would be no need to make any other movement, except to hold the points higher or lower, more inward or outward according to how the enemy finds themselves. When they are outside and very high, one would have to withdraw the hand from third to fourth, maintaining the aforementioned conjunction of arms to defend the angle of the second guard, so that the enemy would not have the opportunity to place the point under the dagger. But when the said enemy is on the inside, one should well maintain the same union, though it would be better nonetheless to have the hand somewhat towards the fourth than in third, for it would be stronger from that side. And if the enemy were to provoke, one should go to strike without making any other defensive motion with the sword, merely extending it to defend.

[ 84 ] GENERAL DISCOURSE On the Use of the Dagger.

[22] (P78.S1)
TO TRAIN ONESELF, WE SAY THAT IT IS GOOD TO HOLD the dagger with the arm extended long, stretched out in front, and slightly aided by the sword, so that the man secures himself in defense and makes small movements in the parries to not expose himself too much on one side while covering the other. For having it extended in front, and disordered with great movements, he would remain confused and struck; therefore, it is necessary to use it judiciously and to become so accustomed through practice that one acquires such precision that in the movements of the sword one does not become disturbed, and that one is always more assured in operating it. Then, once he has achieved this accustomedness and security, he can hold it withdrawn and push it forward in defense according to the occasion. At that time, it will certainly be that he will proceed with more precision to perform his duty and with greater security in overcoming the enemy. Similarly, after such training, he will be able to keep it ever closer to the sword, which will make the defense more convenient and stronger in all operations. The dagger, at that time, will not be so troubled, even if he does not have to hold it firmly in any one position. The reason is that in some cases it is good to have it advanced, and in others, it is not, depending on the situation of the enemy's weapons, sometimes long and sometimes short, and also according to how the same man holds his own guard. Thus, having fixed the body and placed the sword in front, the short dagger will be better; in another case, it will be better advanced, just as in some guards it is better to hold it upright. But if one were to reason about all the positions to show how it should be held in one or the other, or against one or the other position, the discourse would become too lengthy, as the subject is too broad and almost infinite. It will only be said that the point of the dagger, by ordinary rule, should always be held opposite and directed towards the point of the sword held in opposition, until it is turned against the body. When it is out of presence, it would not be appropriate, nor would it be wise to guard it towards the feet, to whom one does not use great diligence in situating the body low enough and comfortably so that one can defend the upper part with little motion, and that one is at the same time ready to strike even while being low. It is cumbersome to operate, as has been said; it is better to cover it with the sword in such a way that the enemy cannot raise it unless he causes it, for this will certainly be safer and of lesser subtlety. The same applies if one has to act against a sword that is very high; one should also hold the dagger so close that there is no space between one hand and the other for the enemy to approach, who, coming forward, would be forced to find the dagger or pass through the strong part of the sword.

[23] (P78.S2)
We must also remember that in the use of the dagger, all defenses must be made with the edge both from within and without, as high as low, and in the strongest part of the dagger, especially against cuts, and also against thrusts when one wishes to slide the edge. It is indeed true that when one intends to strike thrusts, a thing not much praised by us, and even less when passing, it is better to strike them with the point of the dagger at the weak part of the enemy, considering that the point makes a greater circumference.

At that time, when the adversary advances to strike, one can well deliver a thrust or a cut from above, or to the arm of the sword, without doing anything other than widening the left foot back, thus one will save oneself and strike the said enemy.

[86] THIS IS THE SECOND GUARD, AS REGARDS THE ORDER, but nevertheless one of the first in terms of its quality and nature, derived from the other first guard, and the variation that exists between them may have arisen from the man having stepped forward with the left foot, or with the right foot back; there is no other difference between them regarding the position, as far as the greater goodness and safety is concerned, this one is greatly surpassed by the first, because the front leg of this guard presents great danger, and also the side under the dagger is left exposed, and it cannot strike if it does not pass, and if it does pass, it cannot return to the guard without being wounded, both due to the length of the movement, and also for finding oneself so far forward that one can no longer remain within measure; with all this said, this guard can still be used, provided that one observes its proper reasons, which are to wait for the enemy to strike in order to parry and counterstrike, or to feint with the point and strike with the edge; this is quite suitable in this first founded in such a form, similarly to feint with the point and strike with the point, but it would not be of any value if time or opportunity were given to the enemy, when the weight of the body is not placed upon the back leg, leaving the whole front leg exposed, and if while the said enemy comes to strike it, one does not carry it back with the other, so that it would not be difficult to be already unbalanced by the weight, and in this way, with the man remaining bent forward with the body over the right foot, he could strike at the same time with the point or edge according to the opportunity, a blow without comparison better than can be made in this guard.

Let us therefore say that here we shall see the first wound from the sword, which is a dagger, and it is of the fourth, and it wounds one who is in the third guard without any effect of defense or offense. This wound may have been caused because the wounded one has come too close to his enemy, who will have taken that precise moment when he places his foot on the ground to steady himself, and will have wounded him at the point of pause, thus taking him by surprise and depriving him of the ability to do anything. This is a mistake, indeed a folly, of many others still, who usually say, "I was not yet in guard," not realizing that when a man has the sword in hand, he must always be presumed to be in guard. Therefore, one should heed two things: the first is not to advance so much as to stop that the adversary can, at that moment, strike; the second, when stopping, is to ensure that no fall occurs, neither of body, nor of foot, nor of arms. It is necessary to place the foot on the ground without falling, firmly and lightly, with the weapons not far from the spot where the man intends to steady them. Moreover, as one approaches the enemy, one should come adjusting the weapons in such a way that upon reaching the intended mark, those weapons should have no other effect. Thus, by operating in this manner, one may also go to steady oneself at a wide measure, provided that in stopping, one does not fall either with the body or the foot. For even if one were far away and believed he could not be reached by his enemy, he could nonetheless create such a moment that the said enemy would gain some advantage, and at the same time, he would approach him, which would then put him in danger of being wounded while trying to free himself. And even if he were not wounded so suddenly, nonetheless, in that disadvantage, he could be wounded by any slight movement. Therefore, knowing how to go against the enemy is undoubtedly the first warning and of greater consideration, for most often, victory consists in the first advantage.
